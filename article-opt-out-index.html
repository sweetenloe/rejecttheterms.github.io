T&C Red Flags

A living index of terms, privacy language, and the clauses that deserve closer scrutiny.

← Back to articles

This page tracks platform terms, privacy language, and the legal clauses that push too far. Each entry is meant to stay practical: the company, the source links, the language to watch, and the account or privacy controls that matter most.

How this page works

  • Each company gets direct source links to its terms and privacy pages.
  • Questionable clauses are quoted directly once reviewed.
  • Notes focus on what feels coercive, invasive, misleading, or overly broad.
  • Whenever possible, opt-out, deletion, and privacy-control links are listed nearby.

Review criteria

  • Overly broad rights to collect, infer, retain, or share personal data.
  • Forced arbitration, class-action waiver, venue restrictions, or asymmetric remedies.
  • One-sided license grabs over user content, uploads, prompts, or account materials.
  • Dark-pattern friction around deletion, opt-out, tracking controls, or account closure.
  • Terms that can change unilaterally with weak notice or vague consent language.

Current investigations

1. TikTok

Terms: U.S. Terms of Service

Privacy: Privacy Policy

Opt-out / controls: Privacy Center

Status: Reviewed from user-supplied Terms text dated January 22, 2026.

Why it stands out: The current TikTok terms combine broad content licensing, aggressive liability limits, wide discretion to suspend accounts, and ad-personalization language that extends on and off the platform.

Questionable clauses

1. Irrevocable license over your content and AI-related inputs.

Quoted language: By providing Your Content, including messages, prompts, questions, files, Input and Output, you grant TikTok a license that is “non-exclusive, irrevocable, and royalty-free,” “assignable and sub-licensable,” and “worldwide.” TikTok says this includes rights to access, reproduce, distribute, download, adapt, make derivative works, perform, and communicate Your Content to the public for operating, improving, and providing the Platform and “developing new technologies (including training, testing, and improving our machine learning models and algorithms) and services” for TikTok and its service providers and business partners.

Why it matters: This is broad. The license is not just about hosting your post. It reaches prompts, files, and AI interactions, is irrevocable, and expressly covers model training, testing, and improvement, while also extending to service providers and business partners.

2. Other users get a perpetual and irrevocable license too.

Quoted language: Depending on usage and settings, each user of the Platform gets a “non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual and irrevocable, worldwide license” to use Your Content, including to reproduce, distribute, share, download, adapt, make derivative works, perform, and communicate it to the public.

Why it matters: Once content spreads through reposts, remixes, or incorporations into other posts, deletion becomes weaker in practical terms. The terms explicitly warn that deleted content may remain publicly accessible if another user incorporated it into their own content.

3. Ads can be customized on and off platform using third-party information.

Quoted language: TikTok says it may customize your experience, “including the ads you see on and off the Platform.” It also says you agree that it can customize ads and other sponsored content you see on and off the Platform based on, among other points, information it receives from third parties.

Why it matters: This is the kind of wording that expands tracking and profiling beyond the app itself. Even if settings exist, the baseline language is broad and ties personalization to third-party data flows.

4. Your identity can be used in connection with ads you interact with.

Quoted language: TikTok says it may use your name, profile image, and username in connection with ads, sponsored gifts, offers, and other branded or sponsored content that you interact with, without compensation to you.

Why it matters: This turns ordinary engagement into promotional value for the platform and advertisers. Even if framed as enabling platform features, it is still a strong commercial-use permission tied to your identity.

5. Broad moderation and review rights over content and metadata.

Quoted language: TikTok says it can use automated tools and human moderators to review Your Content and associated metadata to identify, detect, and enforce potential or actual violations of the Terms, Community Guidelines, and other policies.

Why it matters: Moderation is expected, but the clause is broad in scope and includes associated metadata, not just what is visibly posted. Combined with private and public content review language elsewhere, it gives TikTok wide inspection room.

6. We may remove or restrict content for any reason.

Quoted language: TikTok says it may remove or restrict access to any content, including publicly or privately posted content, “for any reason,” including policy violations, harm prevention, legal compliance, or where permitted by law.

Why it matters: “For any reason” is expansive. The examples that follow do not meaningfully narrow that first phrase, so the platform keeps maximum discretion.

7. TikTok can suspend or ban accounts at its sole discretion and without prior notice.

Quoted language: TikTok reserves the right, at any time and without prior notice, to ban or suspend your account or restrict feature access “at our sole discretion.”

Why it matters: The appeal path helps somewhat, but the default power allocation is one-sided. The company keeps broad enforcement discretion while the user keeps little guaranteed process.

8. The terms can change, and continued use binds you to the new version.

Quoted language: TikTok says it may make changes from time to time, may sometimes make urgent changes without advance notice, and that if you continue to use the Platform after updated terms are in effect, you agree to be bound by them.

Why it matters: This is common, but still aggressive. It shifts the burden to the user to keep checking legal terms and treats continued use as acceptance, even where advance notice may not happen.

9. One-year deadline to bring claims.

Quoted language: You must initiate any proceeding or action within one year of the event or facts giving rise to the dispute or you “forever waive” the claim and it is “permanently barred.”

Why it matters: This shortens the window users may otherwise expect for legal claims and can function as a quiet rights reduction if users do not notice it.

10. Very aggressive liability cap.

Quoted language: To the extent permitted by law, TikTok’s maximum aggregate liability will not exceed the greater of $100 or the amount you have paid in the past 12 months.

Why it matters: That is a low ceiling relative to the breadth of the platform’s disclaimers, ad ecosystem, moderation discretion, and content-license reach.

Practical notes

  • If you use AI-enabled features, assume prompts, files, input, and output are within the content-license framework described in the terms.
  • Review ad, privacy, and personalization settings carefully, especially anything tied to off-platform activity or third-party information.
  • If you post original work you care about tightly controlling, the irrevocable and sublicensable license language deserves a close read before uploading.
  • If you rely on your account for business or creator work, export what you need and keep local copies. The suspension language is broad and does not promise prior notice.
  • If a dispute ever matters, do not sit on it. The terms impose a one-year limitations period.

2. Meta (Facebook / Instagram)

Terms: Facebook Terms of Service

Privacy: Link pending

Opt-out / controls: Link pending

Status: Reviewed from user-supplied Terms text effective January 1, 2025.

Why it stands out: Meta’s terms lean heavily on cross-product data use, on-and-off-platform ad targeting, broad content licensing, significant discretion over account enforcement, and a one-sided dispute setup that gives Meta more venue flexibility than the user.

Questionable clauses

1. Personalized ads are the price of entry.

Quoted language: Meta says it does not charge you to use Facebook because businesses and organizations pay it to show you ads, and that by using the Products you agree Meta can show you ads it thinks may be relevant to you and your interests using your personal data to help determine which personalized ads to show you.

Why it matters: The terms make clear that surveillance-driven personalization is not peripheral. It is central to the service model. The platform is effectively saying the service is funded by using personal data to decide what ads you get.

2. Data from on and off Meta Products is used to personalize your experience.

Quoted language: Meta says it uses data about the connections you make, the choices and settings you select, and what you share and do “on and off our Products” to personalize your experience.

Why it matters: This is broad cross-context behavioral profiling language. It does not limit personalization to what you do on Facebook itself.

3. Personalized ads and sponsored content can appear on and off Meta Products.

Quoted language: Section 2 says that by using the Meta Products covered by the terms, you agree that Meta can show you personalized ads and other commercial and sponsored content that businesses and organizations pay Meta to promote “on and off” Meta Products.

Why it matters: This extends the advertising model beyond the immediate product environment and reinforces that the data/ads relationship is not confined to what happens only inside Facebook.

4. Data sharing across Meta companies for safety and operational purposes.

Quoted language: Meta says it shares data across Meta Companies when it detects misuse or harmful conduct or to help keep Meta Products, users, and the community safe, and that it may access, preserve, use, and share any information it collects about you where it has a good faith belief it is required or permitted by law to do so.

Why it matters: Safety language can be legitimate, but the clause is broad. It combines internal data sharing with wide preservation and disclosure language where Meta believes the law requires or permits it.

5. Broad content license that is transferable and sublicensable.

Quoted language: When you share, post, or upload IP-covered content, you grant Meta a “non-exclusive, transferable, sub-licensable, royalty-free, and worldwide license” to host, use, distribute, modify, run, copy, publicly perform or display, translate, and create derivative works of your content, consistent with your settings.

Why it matters: This is a classic platform license grab. Meta says it is for providing and improving services, but the list of rights is still broad and includes derivative works and sublicensing.

6. Deletion is slow, conditional, and not always final in practice.

Quoted language: Meta says deletion may take up to 90 days after account or content deletion begins, plus up to another 90 days to remove content from backups and disaster recovery systems. It also says content will not be deleted within 90 days where others used it under the license, technical limitations prevent deletion, or retention is needed for investigations, safety, legal preservation, or government requests.

Why it matters: Users often read “delete” as immediate disappearance. The actual term is much more qualified. The exceptions are broad enough that content can persist for substantial time or longer depending on circumstances.

7. Meta can use your name, profile picture, and actions in connection with ads.

Quoted language: You give Meta permission to use your name and profile picture and information about actions you have taken on Facebook next to or in connection with ads, offers, and other sponsored or commercial content, without compensation.

Why it matters: This turns user identity and engagement into promotional context for advertisers and brands. Even with visibility controls, it is still a strong commercial-use permission.

8. Broad account suspension and deletion discretion.

Quoted language: Meta says it may suspend or permanently disable access, and may permanently disable or delete your account, if it determines in its discretion that you have clearly, seriously, or repeatedly breached its terms or policies. It may also disable or delete your account if it is unused for an extended period, unconfirmed after registration, or if Meta detects someone may have used it without your permission and ownership cannot be confirmed.

Why it matters: The review option is useful, but the enforcement power remains broad. “In our discretion” leaves substantial control with Meta over whether you keep access.

9. Liability cap stays at $100.

Quoted language: Meta’s aggregate liability arising out of or relating to the terms or Meta Products will not exceed the greater of $100 or the amount you have paid in the past twelve months.

Why it matters: The low cap sits alongside sweeping disclaimers and a massive data-driven advertising system. That imbalance is worth flagging.

10. Dispute venue is asymmetric.

Quoted language: You and Meta agree disputes will be resolved exclusively in federal court in the Northern District of California or a state court in San Mateo County, but Meta also says that, in its sole discretion, it may bring claims against you in any competent court in the country in which you reside.

Why it matters: This is one-sided. Your forum is fixed. Meta’s is flexible. That asymmetry is not user-friendly and gives the company procedural advantage.

11. Terms can be updated and continued use binds you.

Quoted language: Meta says it may update the terms to reflect services and practices, safety, security, or legal compliance, and that once updated terms are in effect, you are bound if you continue to access or use the products.

Why it matters: This is standard platform boilerplate, but it still places the burden on the user to monitor changes or leave the service.

12. Feedback can be used without restriction or compensation.

Quoted language: Meta says it may use feedback and suggestions without any restriction or obligation to compensate you, and is under no obligation to keep them confidential.

Why it matters: This is common, but it is still a broad rights grab over user-submitted ideas.

Practical notes

  • Treat Meta’s ad system as deeply tied to behavioral data. The terms themselves say personalization relies on what you do on and off Meta Products.
  • If you care about deletion, remember that the terms describe a multi-stage and exception-heavy removal process, not instant disappearance.
  • If you post original media, read the content-license section carefully. The ownership language sounds friendly, but the operational license remains broad and sublicensable.
  • If account access matters for business, archiving, or messaging, keep backups and recovery routes. Meta reserves broad discretion to disable or delete accounts.
  • If a dispute ever becomes serious, note the venue clause. Meta locks your claims into California while reserving more flexibility for its own claims.
